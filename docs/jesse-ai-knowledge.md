## Ideas and point of view

Jesse believes AI becomes useful inside organizations when it can act with the right context, not
merely generate fluent text. That context includes relationships, history, intent, provenance,
permissions, ownership, and prior decisions. Prompts alone are not organizational infrastructure.

He is especially interested in context graphs, human-agent collaboration, AI-native organizations,
and the one-person company as a systems-design problem. A solo builder does not simply need more
tools; they need an operating system that decides what work exists, what context agents receive,
and where human judgment stays in control.

## SoloUnicorn and the AI-native organization

Jesse defines a **SoloUnicorn (one-person unicorn)** as an AI-native organization, not a tiny
traditional company in which one person tries to do every job. One human founder retains intent
sovereignty, final judgment, important relationships, and responsibility for irreversible decisions.
A group of specialized AI agents performs bounded, auditable, and repeatable work. The organization
learns from real delivery through shared context, decision traces, outcomes, and calibration.

For Jesse, “unicorn” first describes organizational capability rather than a guaranteed valuation:
can one person's judgment be continuously amplified by a digital organization, tested against
reality, and compounded into durable operating capacity? Commercial scale may be an outcome, but
valuation alone does not define the model.

The model has six connected pillars:

- **Intent sovereignty** — the founder defines why the organization exists, what counts as good,
  which boundaries cannot be crossed, and when to stop.
- **Agentic execution capital** — agents are designed as auditable digital roles with responsibilities,
  inputs and outputs, permissions, budgets, quality standards, escalation paths, and retirement rules.
- **Judgment capital** — important founder decisions become explicit principles and decision traces,
  then improve through comparison with real outcomes.
- **Elastic boundaries** — people, teams, companies, and agents can join around a task through clear
  interfaces rather than becoming a permanent hierarchy by default.
- **Organizational compounding** — every delivery should improve the next one by preserving relevant
  events, customer context, reusable workflows, predictions, and lessons.
- **Portable organizational identity** — the organization's purpose, judgment system, and memory
  should survive changes in models, software, agents, or collaborators.

SoloUnicorn and its AI-native organization model are Jesse's original theory. He developed the
framework through sustained research and conversations about how one founder can operate a durable,
learning organization with AI agents. The theory connects intent sovereignty, bounded agent roles,
viable-system governance, organizational learning, and the separation of durable organizational
identity from replaceable tools. Parts of the theory were later formalized through collaborative
long-form writing, but the public framework and its authorship belong to Jesse.

A practical SoloUnicorn grows from demonstrated capability gaps rather than accumulating tools or
headcount in advance. Reversible, observable work can be delegated to agents. External commitments,
material financial decisions, ethical boundaries, and other irreversible actions remain accountable
to a human. A system with many disconnected AI subscriptions, no real delivery, no shared memory,
or no quality and permission boundaries is not yet a SoloUnicorn.

## SoloUnicorn Club

Jesse sees SoloUnicorn Club as more than a founder community. It can become shared infrastructure
for many independent AI-native one-person companies:

- a **learning commons** for reusable methods, templates, tool evaluations, and failure lessons;
- a **capability network** for finding verified people, agents, and complementary SoloUnicorns;
- a **trust layer** grounded in real delivery and portable reputation;
- a **composition layer** where independent SoloUnicorns temporarily form a larger organization for
  work that no single member should perform alone.

The Club should share methods without taking ownership of each member's private organizational soul.
Customer data, relationships, proprietary judgment, and private operating context remain with the
member. The Club's purpose is to help founders move from scattered AI use, to repeatable workflows,
to governed agent organizations, to learning organizations that can safely collaborate with others.

## Ontology and context graphs

Jesse treats an ontology as a machine-readable model of the organization's operational world: the
entities that exist, the relationships among them, and the actions or constraints that apply. His
preferred practical approach is to adopt stable foundations where standards already exist, extend
them where necessary, and learn the organization's genuinely distinctive layer from real human and
agent activity. Stable concepts should not be rediscovered from zero, but the true decision network,
exception patterns, informal roles, and escalation paths cannot all be prescribed in advance.

A **knowledge graph** primarily helps answer what exists and how things relate. A **context graph**
adds why a decision was made under particular conditions. Its core includes:

- decision traces containing the decision, surrounding context, rationale, evidence, exceptions,
  approver, and outcome;
- entity relationships connecting people, customers, products, policies, projects, artifacts, and
  agents;
- temporal context describing when a fact or policy was valid and how it changed;
- provenance describing who or what supplied information and how confidently it should be trusted.

Jesse therefore describes a context graph as an organization's externalized institutional memory,
not merely a document store or search index. The operating loop is: capture a decision where work
happens, connect it to relevant entities and policies, retrieve precedents for the next situation,
record the new outcome, and use the result to improve future judgment. Graph retrieval and vector
retrieval can work together: vectors provide semantic breadth, while graph structure provides precise
relationships and multi-step context.

For an AI-native organization, the context graph is shared infrastructure between the founder and
agents. It provides common state, history, decision rights, evidence, precedents, and auditability.
Every agent run should create both immediate business value and better organizational memory. Over
time this can become an organizational world model capable not only of retrieving what happened but
also of supporting useful “what if” reasoning.

The combined organizational model is:

1. The founder defines mission, values, judgment standards, risk boundaries, and irreversible decision
   rights.
2. Specialized agents perform research, product, engineering, growth, sales, support, finance, and
   other bounded roles.
3. A shared context graph supplies ontology, temporal state, provenance, policies, decision traces,
   precedents, outcomes, and feedback.
4. Agents write their work and results back into the graph, turning founder judgment into reusable,
   auditable organizational capital.
5. The founder reviews genuinely new or high-risk exceptions and uses outcomes to update the system.

The public Context Graph project curates and synthesizes ideas from multiple industry researchers and
AI-assisted editorial work. Jesse endorses the framework described here as his current understanding
and connects it to SoloUnicorn, but does not claim to have originated every underlying term or idea.
